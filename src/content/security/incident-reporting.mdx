# Incident Reporting

One of the most important things you can do for the security of Teams Squared and our clients is to report something suspicious quickly. This module explains what counts as an incident, how to report it, and what happens after you do.

## What Is a Security Incident?

A security incident is any event that has — or could have — a negative impact on the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information or systems.

This includes events that didn't fully materialise but could have ("near-misses"). If something felt wrong or close to wrong, report it anyway.

**Examples of incidents you must report:**

- Loss or theft of your company device
- Suspected malware, ransomware, or virus on your device
- Unauthorised access to a system or account
- A suspected data breach or data sent to the wrong person
- A phishing attempt or credential compromise
- Use of unauthorised software or storage
- Any attempt to bypass or interfere with security controls
- A client raising a security concern involving you or your work
- A device subject to forced access or inspection at a border

## Report Immediately — Without Delay

All incidents must be reported **as soon as you become aware of them**. Do not wait to investigate, confirm, or fix the issue yourself first.

**How to report:**

Email the Cybersecurity Team at **cybersecurity@teamsquared.io**

Your subject line must include the issue type and a brief description:

| Situation | Example Subject Line |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Suspected account compromise | `Password – Suspected Account Compromise` |
| Device lost or stolen | `Device – Laptop Lost in Transit` |
| Phishing email received | `Phishing – Suspicious Email from External Sender` |
| Accidental data disclosure | `Data – File Sent to Wrong Recipient` |
| Malware detected | `Malware – Suspicious Activity on Device` |

For urgent matters, you may also message the Cybersecurity Lead directly in addition to emailing the team address.

## No-Blame Reporting

Teams Squared operates a **no-blame reporting culture**. You will not be penalised for reporting an incident in good faith — even if you were the one who made a mistake.

Delayed or concealed incidents are far harder to contain and cause significantly more damage. Prompt reporting is always the right call.

## What Happens After You Report

Once you report an incident, the Cybersecurity Team takes over. Here is what to expect:

1. **Assessment** — The team will classify the incident by severity: Low, Medium, High, or Critical
2. **Containment** — Depending on the nature, this may involve isolating your device, resetting credentials, revoking access, or other actions
3. **Investigation** — Logs and evidence will be preserved and reviewed
4. **Escalation** — High and Critical incidents are escalated immediately to the ISMS Officer (COO) and senior leadership
5. **Client notification** — If client data or systems may be affected, the relevant client will be notified without undue delay
6. **Recovery** — Once the threat is contained, systems and access are restored securely
7. **Post-incident review** — For Medium, High, and Critical incidents, a review is conducted to identify root causes and prevent recurrence

> **Your role ends at reporting.** Do not attempt to investigate, remediate, or remove malware yourself. Do not discuss the incident with others until the Cybersecurity Team advises you to do so.

## If Your Device Is Compromised or Lost

1. Stop using the device for any work immediately
2. Email cybersecurity@teamsquared.io with the subject `Device – [brief description]`
3. Do not attempt to wipe or repair the device yourself
4. Make yourself available — the Cybersecurity Team may need to remotely lock or wipe the device and will need your cooperation

## If You Receive a Suspicious Email or Phishing Attempt

1. Do not click any links or open any attachments
2. Do not reply to the sender
3. Forward the email to cybersecurity@teamsquared.io with a brief description
4. If you accidentally clicked a link or entered credentials, report it immediately as a potential compromise

## Staying Reachable

While you are on an active work assignment, you must remain **reasonably reachable** for urgent security communications — particularly those involving your device, credentials, or a suspected incident. Check your email and messages regularly and respond promptly to anything from the Cybersecurity Team.
